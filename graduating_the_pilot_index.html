Graduating the Pilot

Why most enterprise AI pilots never become operations, and the four-gate discipline that separates the ones that compound from the ones that stall.

Why this page exists. I prepared this brief alongside my application for the Chief of Staff role at Carrum Health, a company that doubled its covered lives in a single year and has committed to extending its AI foundation across every link in the care chain. Rather than describe how I approach an ambiguous operating problem, I decided to show it. A company whose North Star is trusted access to high-quality care at lower cost needs its AI adoption governed by that same star. What follows is the shape of that governance: the diagnosis, the options, a scorecard you can use, and where the discipline breaks.

The bottom line

Pilots do not die at the technology layer. They die at the moment of graduation, when a tool stops being an experiment and starts changing who is accountable for what. For a company whose product is trust, every workflow that gets automated either extends that promise or quietly rebuilds the friction the company exists to remove.

The core challenge

Graduation changes the physics

A pilot is a tool nobody's job depends on. A rollout changes whose judgment counts, who answers when the output is wrong, and whose daily work gets restructured. When teams resist adoption, the standard diagnosis is skepticism and the standard prescription is training. Both miss the mechanism: people resist because no one has told them where their accountability now sits. Until that question has an answer, resistance is not a culture problem. It is due diligence performed by the people closest to the risk.

Pilot

  • StakesLow. Voluntary. Reversible by anyone.
  • AccountabilityDiffuse. The experiment owns its own errors.
  • JudgmentUnchanged. The tool advises, people decide as before.
  • Blast radiusOne team, contained.
The graduation line

Rollout

  • StakesOperational. Members feel the output.
  • AccountabilityMust be named, or it dissolves.
  • JudgmentRedistributed. Someone's expertise is now mediated.
  • Blast radiusEvery business unit, multiplied by every member.

Everything that matters changes at the line, and none of it is technical. Healthcare has already run the careless version of this experiment: automated prior authorization triggered a volume arms race between payers and providers, and patients absorbed the delay. AI amplifies whatever a workflow was already designed to do. Deployed into workflows built to widen access, it widens access. Deployed as a cost shortcut into workflows nobody re-examined, it becomes the new gatekeeping.

The recommendation · Try it

The graduation scorecard

Four gates a pilot must clear before enterprise rollout, reviewed on a standing cadence rather than ad hoc enthusiasm. Score a hypothetical pilot and watch the verdict respond. One gate outranks the others.

Gate 1 · Accountability Post-rollout ownership has a name attached. A person, not a committee, with authority to modify or kill the workflow. If no one will sign, that reluctance is information.
Gate 2 · Enhancement The people whose work changes affirm the tool extends their judgment rather than replaces it. Their assessment, not their manager's. A pilot the operators would not keep has not passed.
Gate 3 · Measurement Success was defined before rollout, and it measures outcomes, not usage. Cycle time to the member, error catch points, and whether humans now exercise more judgment on harder problems.
Gate 4 · Access Post-automation, it is easier for a member to reach the care the network was built to deliver, measured from the member's side of the interaction. In a trust-based model, this gate is a veto.
Verdict
Score all four gates
The verdict updates as you decide. Skeptics are not overridden along the way; the strongest objection raised in each pilot becomes a design constraint recorded in the review. That is what psychological safety means operationally.
A well-documented kill is celebrated like a graduation. The kill memo is as valuable as the launch memo.
Friction analysis

Where this fails in practice

A discipline that cannot name its own failure modes is theater. Four to design against from day one.

Failure modeCountermeasure
The middle squeeze. Managers between leadership enthusiasm and team anxiety pass pilots that should die, because killing one reads as resistance. Make the well-run kill a celebrated outcome, not a career risk.
Metric drift. Under quarterly pressure, outcome metrics quietly revert to usage metrics. Fix success definitions before the pilot starts, in writing.
Speed pressure. Gates get waived for pilots with executive sponsors, converting governance into theater. No waivers without a name attached. Someone signs either way.
Calcification. The discipline succeeds, then accretes into bureaucracy that kills velocity. The gates review themselves on a schedule, or they become the next workaround.
How this was built

I built this brief with AI as an active collaborator, in the same working pattern it recommends, the way I have worked daily for the past two years. AI handled research synthesis, structural drafting, and stress-testing of the argument. Human judgment made the calls machines should not: the diagnosis that adoption failure is an authority problem rather than a technology problem, the decision that the access gate holds a veto in a trust-based model, and the friction analysis, which comes from years of watching organizations misattribute structural failures to the people absorbing them.

The boundary between what was delegated and what was decided is the point. It is the same boundary the four gates exist to protect.

One note on scope: the full graduation review design, the cadence, the templates, and the metric architecture behind these gates, is the work itself. This page is its shape.
